GDP per Capita

Source: World Bank — NY.GDP.PCAP.CD

Gross Domestic Product per capita in current US dollars measures average material living standards. GDP per capita is a mean — it does not reveal how income is distributed across the population.

Gini Coefficient

Source: World Bank — SI.POV.GINI

The Gini coefficient measures income inequality within a country on a scale of 0 (perfect equality) to 100 (all income to one person). These figures derive from household surveys on post-transfer disposable income. Background shading marks Low (<27), Moderate (27–36), and High (>36) inequality bands.

Income Share by Quintile

Source: World Bank — SI.DST.* series

Income distributed across five equally-sized population groups. The wider the gap between the top and bottom quintile shares, the greater the income spread. Countries are sorted by bottom-quintile share (most equal at top).

Pre-Tax National Income Shares

Source: World Inequality Database — wid.world, 2022

Top income concentration from the World Inequality Database, built on tax records and national accounts — capturing market income before redistribution. These figures are typically higher than post-transfer survey Gini measures, revealing the raw concentration of economic output.

Top 1% — Income Share vs Wealth Share

Source: WID.world (income) · Credit Suisse GWR 2023 (wealth)

Wealth is almost always more concentrated than income. This chart shows — for each country — how the top 1%'s share of pre-tax income (circle) compares to their share of total net wealth (diamond). The gap between the two reveals how capital accumulation amplifies inequality beyond what income alone suggests.

Lorenz Curves

Source: Parametric log-normal · World Bank Gini

The Lorenz curve plots the cumulative income share earned by the bottom x% of the population. The further the curve bows below the 45° equality line, the greater the inequality — and the larger the Gini coefficient. Curves are derived from a log-normal model fitted to each country's Gini; focal countries are shaded.

Income Distribution Density

Source: Parametric log-normal model

Estimated probability density of income using a log-normal model calibrated to each country's Gini and GDP per capita. A narrow, tall peak signals a compressed distribution; a long right tail signals high inequality. Focal countries shown with filled areas; comparison countries as dashed lines.

Wealth Concentration

Source: Credit Suisse Global Wealth Report 2023 · WID.world

Net personal wealth is significantly more concentrated than income. These figures show what share of total household wealth is held by the wealthiest 1%, the top 10%, and the bottom half of the population. Countries sorted by top 1% wealth share.

Wealth Skewness — Mean vs Median

Source: Credit Suisse Global Wealth Report 2023

When mean wealth greatly exceeds median wealth, the distribution is right-skewed — a small number of ultra-wealthy individuals pull the average far above the typical person's holdings. The mean/median ratio (right panel) directly quantifies this skew.

Gini vs GDP per Capita

Source: World Bank · OECD

Does higher income mean lower inequality? Not automatically. Bubble size represents the relative poverty rate. The Nordic countries demonstrate that high prosperity and low inequality can coexist — challenging the notion of an inevitable trade-off.

Relative Poverty Rates

Source: OECD Income Distribution Database, ~2021

The share of the population with income below 50% of the national median — the OECD's standard relative poverty threshold. This captures how many people fall significantly below the living standards of their own society.

Income Distribution Model

The Lorenz curves and income density charts use a parametric log-normal model. For a log-normal distribution, the Gini G satisfies G = 2Φ(σ/√2) − 1, where Φ is the standard normal CDF. Given observed Gini G and mean income M: σ = √2 · Φ⁻¹((G+1)/2) and μ = ln(M) − σ²/2. Note: log-normal distributions underestimate the extreme upper tail of real income distributions, which empirically follow a Pareto distribution above the ~90th percentile. These charts are illustrative of distributional shape, not precise estimates of tail income.

Country Selection

The United States, Canada, and Norway are the primary focus. Five additional OECD peer nations (Sweden, Denmark, Germany, United Kingdom, France) are included to provide distributional context and anchor the relative position of focal countries within the high-income world.

Limitations

  • World Bank Gini figures rely on household surveys, which systematically under-capture top incomes. WID figures using tax records typically show higher concentration.
  • Wealth data comparability is limited by differences in national measurement methodologies, pension treatment, and survey coverage.
  • Data vintages vary across indicators and countries; all comparisons are approximate.
  • GDP per capita is used as a proxy for mean household income in the log-normal model; this slightly overstates true mean household income.
